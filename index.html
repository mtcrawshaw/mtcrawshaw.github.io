About Me

I am a second year Ph.D. student in Computer Science at George Mason University under Professor Jana Kosecka. My research focuses on multi-task learning and meta-learning in computer vision and robotics. Before studying at George Mason, I received a B.S. in mathematics and computer science from Ohio State University.

Generally, I am interested in building machine learning algorithms that can transfer knowledge across very wide task distributions, which humans can do to a great extent. When a human toddler is learning basic motor control from scratch, they are able to learn tasks - such as twisting a door knob and grasping a bottle in hand - in a way that allows them to distill fundamental skills from these tasks. These skills can be transferred to quickly perform new but related tasks, such as simultaneously grasping a bottle and twisting that bottle's cap open. Current machine learning methods aren't able to perform such general transfer of knowledge across tasks in motor control or any other domain, and my goal is to build systems that can.

Current Projects

  • Multi-Task Learning and Meta-Learning for Robotic Manipulation: Meta-World is a recently proposed benchmark suite for multi-task learning and meta-learning within the domain of robotic manipulation. Although each of the 50 individual manipulation tasks (open a drawer, push an object, etc) are easily solved with an off-the-shelf reinforcement learning algorithm such as Soft Actor-Critic, learning the tasks simultaneously with a single model or transferring knowledge from previous to unseen tasks has proven a challenge for existing methods. I am currently approaching this challenge (both multi-task learning and meta-learning benchmarks) with modular neural networks (see Modular Meta-Learning) that ease the multi-task optimization and allow for compositional generalization through recombination of modules.
